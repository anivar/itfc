\n\t\t\t\t\t\tAccess to Justice Issues in Cases of Online Gender-Based Violence\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/h1>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t

\n\t\t\t\t\t\tThis module highlights the various challenges and barriers faced by survivors of online gender-based violence (OGBV) in different stages of their pursuit of access to justice, beginning from filing of a police complaint, to the stages leading to trial and judgment by court. These challenges or barriers call for greater sensitivity by courts in adjudicating cases of online violence in order to ensure an effective remedy for the survivors. The module also provides some suggestions for courts on what they can do within their powers to ameliorate the access-to-justice issues faced by the survivors. \t\t\t\t\t\t<\/p>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n<\/div><\/div><\/div>

\n\t\t\t\t\t\t3.1 What does Access to Justice Mean?\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/h2>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t\t
<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n<\/div><\/div><\/div>
\n
\n\t

Access to justice is an invaluable human right, which is also recognized in most constitutional democracies as a fundamental right.1<\/a><\/sup>Anita Kushwaha and Ors. v. Pushap Sudan and Ors<\/em>, AIR 2016 SC 3506, order dated 19 July 2016, Supreme Court of India.\u00a0<\/span> It is a basic principle of the rule of law. Access to justice is an important prerequisite for the effective enjoyment and protection of other traditional as well as new social rights.2<\/a><\/sup>Rabel, C. (1978). Access to Justice: A World Survey <\/i>(1) Giuffre-sijthoff And Noordhoff. <\/span> Hence, it is seen as the most \u201cbasic human right\u201d,3<\/a><\/sup>Rabel, C. (1978). Access to Justice: A World Survey <\/i>(1) Giuffre-sijthoff And Noordhoff. <\/span> and recognized as such by international human rights treaties, including the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)4<\/a><\/sup>Universal Declaration of Human Rights<\/em> (entered into force 1948). https:\/\/www.ohchr.org\/en\/universal-declaration-of-human-rights<\/a> <\/span> and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR), 1966.5<\/a><\/sup>International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, opened for signature 1966 (entered into force 23 March 1976). https:\/\/www.equalityhumanrights.com\/en\/our-human-rights-work\/monitoring-and-promoting-un-treaties\/international-covenant-civil-and<\/a><\/span><\/p>\n

Article 8 of the UDHR provides that everyone has the right to an effective remedy by competent national tribunals for acts violating the fundamental rights granted to them by the Constitution or by law. Article 10 of the Declaration recognizes the right of everyone to be entitled, in full equality, to a fair and public hearing by an independent and impartial tribunal. The same rights relating to access to justice are also recognized by Clause 3 of Article 2 of the ICCPR.<\/p>\n

In India, the Supreme Court has recognized access to justice as a guaranteed fundamental right under the Constitution, particularly in relation to Article 21, which guarantees the right to life and personal liberty.6<\/a><\/sup>Imtiyaz Ahmad v. State of Uttar Pradesh and Ors, <\/i>(2012) 2 SCC 688.<\/span> Through several decisions, the court has recognized different aspects of access to justice. In Hussainara Khatoon<\/i> v.<\/i> State of Bihar (1979)<\/i>,7<\/a><\/sup>Hussainara Khatoon v. Home Secretary, State of Bihar<\/em>, (1980) 1 SCC 81.<\/span> the right to speedy trial was recognized as an integral and essential part of the fundamental right to life and liberty enshrined in Article 21.<\/p>\n

Drawing upon the Directive Principle of State Policy under Article 39A, the court has, in several cases, recognized the right to free legal aid and legal representation as a fundamental right implicit in, and an inalienable element of reasonable, fair, and just procedure under Article 21. In Mohammed Ajmal Mohammad Amir Kasab and Ors<\/i>. v.<\/i> State of Maharashtra (2012)<\/i>,8<\/a><\/sup>Mohammed Ajmal Mohammad Amir Kasab and Ors. v. State of Maharashtra<\/em>, (2012) 9 SCC 234.<\/span> the court held that the right to access legal aid is present even at the pre-trial stage.<\/p>\n

In Imtiyaz Ahmed<\/i> v. State of Uttar Pradesh and Ors (2012)<\/i>, the Supreme Court held that access to justice implicitly includes the right of access to an independent judiciary. In the same case, the court also expanded the scope of this right. It held that access to justice is much more than improving an individual's access to courts, or guaranteeing representation, and must be defined in terms of ensuring that legal and judicial outcomes are just and equitable. In so doing, the court relied on the United Nations Development Programme, Access to Justice Practice Note (2004), which identifies several barriers to realizing effective access to justice. These include prohibitive costs of using the legal system and long delays, the lack of effective legal aid and affordable legal representation, abuse of authority and power, limitations in existing remedies provided by law, gender bias in law and legal systems, the lack of legal awareness, avoidance of the legal system due to a perceived futility of purpose, etc.9<\/a><\/sup>United Nations Development Program. (2004). Access to Justice Practice Note.<\/i> https:\/\/www.undp.org\/sites\/g\/files\/zskgke326\/files\/publications\/Justice_PN_En.pdf<\/a> <\/span><\/p>\n

The above discussion indicates that effective access to justice is not only a matter of access to courts and speedy trial, rather it is also concerned with improving access to laws and law enforcement agencies such as the police.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n<\/div><\/div>

3.1.1 Access to justice in cases of crimes against women<\/h3>\n
\n\t

Victims of crime, particularly women, face many barriers to accessing justice and getting redressal for the violation of their rights. Often, women find it difficult to even approach the police and register a First Information Report (FIR). This difficulty was noted by a Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court in Lalita Kumari<\/i> v.<\/i> Government of Uttar Pradesh (2013)<\/i>.10<\/a><\/sup>Lalita Kumari v. Government of Uttar Pradesh<\/em>, (2014) 2 SCC 1.\u00a0 <\/span> Access to justice in terms of affordable and effective legal aid and advice as well as adequate and equal representation are also problems that victims have to contend with. Under the Legal Services Authority Act, 1987, women can receive free legal services to file or defend a case,11<\/a><\/sup>The Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987. Section 12. <\/span> but this is conditional upon the satisfaction of the concerned Legal Services Authority that the woman has a prima facie case to prosecute or defend.12<\/a><\/sup>The Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987. Section 13. <\/span><\/p>\n

During trials, women often undergo revictimization through repeated courtroom appearances, and aggressive and intimidating cross-examinations.13<\/a><\/sup>Satish, M. (2016). Discretion, Discrimination and the Rule of Law: Reforming Rape Sentencing in India<\/i>. Cambridge University Press. <\/span> This issue was flagged as a serious concern by the Supreme Court in Mallikarjun Kodagali (Dead) represented through Legal Representatives<\/i> v.<\/i> State of Karnataka and Ors (2018).<\/i>14<\/a><\/sup>Mallikarjun Kodagali (Dead) represented through Legal Representatives v. State of Karnataka and Ors, <\/i>AIR 2018 SC 5206. <\/span> The court also observed that while the courts are able to provide some solace to the victims through the victim compensation scheme, it is not adequate and that the judiciary is obliged to go beyond merely awarding compensation and take into consideration the larger picture from the perspective of the victim, which could even include issues relating to infrastructure in court buildings.15<\/a><\/sup>Mallikarjun Kodagali (Dead) represented through Legal Representatives v. State of Karnataka and Ors<\/i>, AIR 2018 SC 5206, at para 4. <\/span> Even the victim compensation scheme as is currently implemented has many shortcomings: inadequate compensation, lack of uniformity in the amount of compensation across states in India, lack of awareness, poor budgetary allocation by states, cumbersome and rigid procedures to obtain compensation, and focus on offline grave offences.16<\/a><\/sup>Menon, V. (n.d.). Revisiting Victim Compensation in India<\/i>. Manupatra. http:\/\/docs.manupatra.in\/newsline\/articles\/Upload\/6F5E12E5-2A56-49A9-BF1B-CBE1DF4F8726.2-F__criminal.pdf<\/a> <\/span><\/p>\n

It is useful to refer here to the Declaration of the Basic Principles of Justice for Victims of Crime and Abuse of Power, adopted by the United Nations General Assembly,17<\/a><\/sup>OHCHR. (1885). Declaration of Basic Principles of Justice for Victims of Crime and Abuse of Power<\/i>. Principle 4. General Assembly Resolution, 40\/34.\u00a0https:\/\/www.ohchr.org\/en\/instruments-mechanisms\/instruments\/declaration-basic-principles-justice-victims-crime-and-abuse<\/a> <\/span> which has also been endorsed by the Supreme Court of India.18<\/a><\/sup>Mallikarjun Kodagali (Dead) represented through Legal Representatives v. State of Karnataka and Ors, <\/i>AIR 2018 SC 5206. <\/span> The Declaration provides that victims of crimes \u201cshould be treated with compassion and respect for their dignity\u201d.19<\/a><\/sup>OHCHR. (1885). Declaration of Basic Principles of Justice for Victims of Crime and Abuse of Power<\/i>. Principle 4. General Assembly Resolution, 40\/34.\u00a0https:\/\/www.ohchr.org\/en\/instruments-mechanisms\/instruments\/declaration-basic-principles-justice-victims-crime-and-abuse<\/a> <\/span> It also states that judicial and administrative mechanisms should be strengthened and made responsive to enable victims to obtain redress. Responsiveness is facilitated by informing victims about the details of their case proceedings; ensuring their participation and giving due importance to their views and concerns; providing proper assistance to victims throughout the process; taking measures to minimize inconveniences, protecting their privacy, and ensuring the safety of both victims and witnesses; and avoiding unnecessary delays.20<\/a><\/sup>OHCHR. (1985). Declaration of Basic Principles of Justice for Victims of Crime and Abuse of Power<\/i>. General Assembly Resolution 40\/34. https:\/\/www.ohchr.org\/en\/instruments-mechanisms\/instruments\/declaration-basic-principles-justice-victims-crime-and-abuse<\/a> <\/span><\/p>\n<\/div>\n<\/div><\/div><\/div>

\n\t\t\t\t\t\t3.2 Access to Justice Issues in OGBV Cases\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/h2>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t\t
<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n<\/div><\/div><\/div>
\n
\n\t

This section highlights some of the hurdles that women victims of online gender-based violence (OGBV) face while pursuing remedy. These hurdles are not confined to access to the legal system (police, courts, and other authorities) in terms of existence of laws, availability of legal aid and counsel, the process of adjudication, enforcement, etc. Rather, a broad look at access to justice issues should recognize and address \"the structural\" inequalities between men and women, the systemic and historic disadvantaged position of women, the culture of impunity in cases of violence against women, and the prevailing gender bias and system of patriarchy that continue to persist in the judicial system and in society.\u201d21<\/a><\/sup>OHCHR. (2010). Understanding Women\u2019s Access to Justice: A Briefer on Women\u2019s Access to Justice<\/i>. https:\/\/www.ohchr.org\/sites\/default\/files\/documents\/HRBodies\/CEDAW\/AccesstoJustice\/WomensLegalAndHumanRightsBureau.pdf<\/a>. <\/span><\/p>\n

Adopting such a broad view of access to justice, the following subsections will discuss the barriers that victims of OGBV face in every stage of the pursuit of justice \u2013 from the time of the incident of crime and the filing of a police complaint, to the investigation, the trial, and the eventual adjudication by the court.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n<\/div><\/div>

3.2.1 At the time of filing complaints<\/h3>\n
\n\t

3.2.1.1 Hesitancy in filing complaints<\/h4>\n

Most OGBV cases either go unreported in India, or are reported to public authorities with significant delays.22<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., et al. (2019). Born Digital, Born Free?: A Socio-Legal Study on Young Women\u2019s Experiences of Online Violence in South India<\/i>, pp. 23-26. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/1662\/Born-Digital_Born-Free_SynthesisReport.pdf<\/a><\/span> An IT for Change study found that a mere 10% of the respondents, who were survivors of technology-facilitated gender-based violence (TFGBV), sought help from the police,23<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., et al. (2019). Born Digital, Born Free?: A Socio-Legal Study on Young Women\u2019s Experiences of Online Violence in South India<\/i>, pp. 23-26. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/1662\/Born-Digital_Born-Free_SynthesisReport.pdf<\/a> <\/span> indicating a hesitancy among women in reporting such crimes. The reasons for the hesitancy range from the stigma associated with sexual crimes to the fear of injustice. Some of these causes are explored below:<\/p>\n

Stigma and further harassment<\/h5>\n

In India, the shame and stigma associated with sexual violence affects victims more than perpetrators. It is women as victims\/survivors who are blamed for sexual violence, which is reflected in comments like \u201cshe asked for it\u201d.24<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N. (2018). Examining Technology-Mediated Violence against Women through a Feminist Framework: Towards Appropriate Legal-Institutional Responses in India<\/em>, pp. 23-26. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/e-vaw\/wp-content\/uploads\/2018\/03\/ITFC-DISCUSSION-PAPER.pdf<\/a>.<\/span> Such stigma is also at work during the filing and follow-up of a police complaint, due to \u201cdeep-rooted patriarchal cultures within institutions of law enforcement that manifest either as unfriendliness or as patronizing protectionism\u201d.25<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N. (2018). Examining Technology-Mediated Violence against Women through a Feminist Framework: Towards Appropriate Legal-Institutional Responses in India<\/em>, pp. 23-26. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/e-vaw\/wp-content\/uploads\/2018\/03\/ITFC-DISCUSSION-PAPER.pdf<\/a> <\/span> Besides the negative perception associated with having faced a gender-based crime, there is a stigma associated with being involved in a police case.26<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N. (2018). Examining Technology-Mediated Violence against Women through a Feminist Framework: Towards Appropriate Legal-Institutional Responses in India<\/em>, pp. 23-26. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/e-vaw\/wp-content\/uploads\/2018\/03\/ITFC-DISCUSSION-PAPER.pdf<\/a><\/span> Often, people close to a survivor distance themselves when the person decides to seek legal recourse. Fear of stigma is, therefore, a major factor that stops women from reporting OGBV. Many survivors resort to alternative and worrying options, such as reducing their use of and engagement on platforms or tailoring their online personas to avoid abuse.27<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N. (2018). Examining Technology-Mediated Violence against Women through a Feminist Framework: Towards Appropriate Legal-Institutional Responses in India<\/em>, pp. 23-26. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/e-vaw\/wp-content\/uploads\/2018\/03\/ITFC-DISCUSSION-PAPER.pdf<\/a><\/span><\/p>\n

Survivors of OGBV also hesitate to file complaints due to fear of intimidation and revictimization. It is common for offenders to resort to intimidation to prevent survivors from reporting a crime, or, in cases where a report has already been filed, to dissuade them from pursuing legal action.28<\/a><\/sup>Malimath, V.S. (Eds). (2003). Report (Volume 1) of the Committee on Reforms of Criminal Justice System<\/i>. Ministry of Home Affairs, Government of India. https:\/\/www.mha.gov.in\/sites\/default\/files\/criminal_justice_system.pdf<\/a> .<\/span> During the #MeToo movement, for instance, several prominent and powerful people accused of sexual misconduct filed defamation cases against complainants, which was widely considered to be an intimidation tactic.29<\/a><\/sup> Human Rights Watch. (2020). \u201cNo #MeToo for Women Like Us\u201d: Poor Enforcement of India\u2019s Sexual Harassment Law. <\/i>https:\/\/www.hrw.org\/report\/2020\/10\/14\/no-metoo-women-us\/poor-enforcement-indias-sexual-harassment-law<\/a>; Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N. (2018). Examining Technology-Mediated Violence against Women through a Feminist Framework: Towards Appropriate Legal-Institutional Responses in India<\/em>, pp. 25-26. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/e-vaw\/wp-content\/uploads\/2018\/03\/ITFC-DISCUSSION-PAPER.pdf<\/a>. \u00a0<\/span> Revictimization happens when survivors are subjected to threatening messages via technology-facilitated devices, which is also a form of OGBV.30<\/a><\/sup>Bansal, V., et.al. (2023). A Scoping Review of Technology-Facilitated Gender-Based Violence in Low- and Middle-Income Countries Across Asia.<\/i> Trauma, Violence, & Abuse<\/i>. https:\/\/doi.org\/10.1177\/15248380231154614<\/a>. <\/span> It is natural for persons who have faced severe verbal or emotional abuse online to be afraid of the physical consequences of filing formal complaints.<\/p>\n

Power imbalances<\/h5>\n

Survivors often hold very little power at various levels of the legal process and system. This not only fuels gender-based violence but also deters women from reporting these crimes. The power imbalance is exacerbated in cases where there are other intersecting inequalities between perpetrators and survivors, such as in terms of caste, religion, class, etc.<\/p>\n

For instance, in cases where the perpetrator is a popular person, like an actor or a politician, the survivor fears not only intimidation but also that the blame would be shifted to her. Powerful abusers often easily evade legal punishment using their resources and influence. They are also protected by digital platforms whose content moderation mechanisms penalize marginalized groups and safeguard people in power.31<\/a><\/sup>Mital, A. (2023). Moderating Bodies: Reproducing Systems of Power through Platform Policies. In A. Tewari & A. Dasarathy (Eds.), Feminist Perspectives on Social Media Governance, (p. 55). IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2291\/ITFC_Feminist%20Perspectives%20on%20Social%20Media%20Governance_0.pdf<\/a><\/span><\/p>\n

Other instances of unequal power manifest where the perpetrator is the survivor\u2019s teacher or supervisor, in which case the fear of consequences to their education stops survivors from filing complaints.32<\/a><\/sup>Krishnan, K. (2015). St Stephen's Sexual Harassment Case Reveals How DU Can Ruin a Student.<\/i> DailyO. https:\/\/www.dailyo.in\/politics\/st-stephens-college-sexual-harassment-phd-student-valson-thampu-4752<\/a> <\/span> The List of Sexual Harassers in Academia, that was compiled by Raya Sarkar in 2017, revealed the pervasiveness of such abuse in the country.33<\/a><\/sup>Krishnan, K. (2015). St Stephen's Sexual Harassment Case Reveals How DU Can Ruin a Student.<\/i> DailyO. https:\/\/www.dailyo.in\/politics\/st-stephens-college-sexual-harassment-phd-student-valson-thampu-4752<\/a><\/span> That several women agreed to anonymously call out abusive professors through Sarkar\u2019s list reveals that the fear of repercussions has a strong hold over survivors in cases where there is a significant power imbalance between them and their abusers.34<\/a><\/sup>Bhandara, V. (2017). Raya Sarkar's List of Academic 'Predators' is the Disruptor we Needed in Sexual Harassment Discourse. <\/i>Firstpost. https:\/\/www.firstpost.com\/india\/raya-sarkars-list-of-academic-predators-is-the-disruptor-we-needed-in-sexual-harassment-discourse-4179573.html<\/a> <\/span><\/p>\n

Power imbalance within a household can also impact the reporting of a crime. The decision to file complaints is often not the survivors\u2019 alone, but rather influenced by other members of a family. Often, the most power in a family unit is held by male members and\/or the elders, who may overrule the survivor\u2019s decision to seek legal recourse,35<\/a><\/sup>Kovacs, A. (2013). Don\u2019t Let it Stand!\u2019 An Exploratory Study of Women and Verbal Online Abuse in India. <\/i>Internet Democracy Project. https:\/\/internetdemocracy.in\/reports\/women-and-verbal-online-abuse-in-india<\/a>. <\/span> due to the purported dishonor it would bring. Further, parents may cite fear for the safety of the survivor and the rest of the family in deciding against filing a complaint, especially if the abuser is powerful.36<\/a><\/sup>Devika, J., et al. (2019). Walking on Eggshells: A study on Gender Justice and Women's Struggles in Malayali <\/i>Cyberspace.<\/i> IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/1618\/Kerala-Report_Righting-Gender-Norms.pdf<\/a><\/span><\/p>\n

Fear of counterintuitive outcomes<\/h5>\n

Finally, the fear of punitive measures is a strong cause for under-reporting OGBV. Many survivors do not report their experiences of OGBV to their families for fear that their phones would get taken away,37<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N. (2018). Examining Technology-Mediated Violence against Women through a Feminist Framework: Towards Appropriate Legal-Institutional Responses in India<\/em>, pp. 23-26. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/e-vaw\/wp-content\/uploads\/2018\/03\/ITFC-DISCUSSION-PAPER.pdf<\/a>.<\/span> or that they would be asked to stop using such platforms thereon.38<\/a><\/sup>Kovacs, A. (2013). Don\u2019t Let it Stand!\u2019 An Exploratory Study of Women and Verbal Online Abuse in India. <\/i>Internet Democracy Project. https:\/\/internetdemocracy.in\/reports\/women-and-verbal-online-abuse-in-india<\/a>. <\/span> There are many instances of sexual violence survivors being married away at an early age for fear that they will not find suitable grooms.39<\/a><\/sup>Human Rights Watch. (2017). \u201cEveryone Blames Me\u201d: Barriers to Justice and Support Services for Sexual Assault Survivors in India.<\/i> https:\/\/www.hrw.org\/sites\/default\/files\/report_pdf\/india1117_web.pdf<\/a>. <\/span> Apprehensions about such outcomes keep women from reporting their experience to close family members and, by extension, to formal authorities.<\/p>\n

Survivors are also skeptical of their chances of getting justice. Many fear that approaching courts will lead to delays and expenses that they do not wish to, or cannot, bear.40<\/a><\/sup>Devika, J., et al. (2019). Walking on Eggshells: A Study on Gender Justice and Women's Struggles in Malayali Cyberspace.<\/i> IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/1618\/Kerala-Report_Righting-Gender-Norms.pdf<\/a> <\/span> Especially in cases of OGBV occurring in the online public sphere, the difficulty of locating abusers, among other things, leads to significant expenses and delays in resolving cases. The added fear of ridicule, stigmatization, and revictimization in police stations and during court proceedings, and the possibility of intimidation by powerful abusers,41<\/a><\/sup>Devika, J., et al. (2019). Walking on Eggshells: A Study on Gender Justice and Women's Struggles in Malayali <\/i>Cyberspace.<\/i> IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/1618\/Kerala-Report_Righting-Gender-Norms.pdf<\/a> <\/span> prompt many survivors, especially those who personally know senior police officers,42<\/a><\/sup>Devika, J., et al. (2019). Walking on Eggshells: A Study on Gender Justice and Women's Struggles in Malayali <\/i>Cyberspace.<\/i> IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/1618\/Kerala-Report_Righting-Gender-Norms.pdf<\/a> <\/span> to opt for out-of-court settlements.<\/p>\n

3.2.1.2 Lack of support and knowledge gaps in filing complaints<\/h4>\n
Lack of access to knowledge, information, and rights<\/h5>\n

Victims of gender-based violence and OGBV are often not taken seriously by law enforcement agencies,43<\/a><\/sup>Human Rights Watch. (2017). \u201cEveryone Blames Me\u201d: Barriers to Justice and Support Services for Sexual Assault Survivors in India, <\/i>pp. 22-32. https:\/\/www.hrw.org\/sites\/default\/files\/report_pdf\/india1117_web.pdf<\/a>; Gurumurthy, A., et al. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/1662\/Born-Digital_Born-Free_SynthesisReport.pdf<\/a> <\/span> and thus lack support in the very first step of accessing justice, namely, filing a police complaint.<\/p>\n

Many women, despite being digitally literate, are unaware of the criminal nature of OGBV. The complicated nature of cybercrime laws \u2013 and OGBV itself \u2013 means that survivors also find it difficult to ascertain the type of violence they have faced and whether it has crossed legal thresholds to merit complaints or it is a silly<\/i>, dismissible prank.44<\/a><\/sup>Devika, J., et al.     3.2.2.1 Collection of evidence<\/h4>\n

    As with all criminal cases, evidence is crucial to prove a crime of OGBV beyond reasonable doubt.     In cases of OGBV, particularly non-consensual intimate image distribution (NCIID), voyeurism, stalking, etc., our study found an unfortunate trend of the police preferring to deploy anti-obscenity Sections (Section 292 of the IPC, and Sections 67 and 67A of the IT Act), rather than provisions rooted in privacy and consent (Sections 354C and 354D of the IPC and Section 66E of the IT Act).96<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N. (2019). Born Digital, Born Free? A Socio-Legal Study on Young Women\u2019s Experiences of Online Violence in South India<\/i>, pp. 26-29. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/1662\/Born-Digital_Born-Free_SynthesisReport.pdf<\/a>.<\/span><\/p>\n

    The police also justifies the invocation of obscenity provisions on the grounds that they help cope with the mutations in cyberviolence.103<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N.     3.2.3.1 Framing of charges<\/h4>\n

    Framing of charges is a crucial stage where the court, based on the police report and other supporting documents presented by the prosecution, states the offense and the relevant provision(s) under which the accused is charged and will be put to trial.104<\/a><\/sup>Pillai, K.N.C. (1980). R.V. Kelkar\u2019s Lectures on Criminal Procedure including Probation and Juvenile Justice<\/i>, pp. 94-95. Eastern Book Company.<\/span> This stage allows the court to review the charge sheet filed by the police and apply its judicial mind to whether or not there are grounds to proceed under the Sections invoked by the police.105<\/a><\/sup>Union of India v. Prafulla Kumar Samal,<\/em> (1979) 3 SCC 4, at para 7.<\/span> The Supreme Court in Union of India v. Prafulla Kumar Samal<\/i> (1978), observed that the court, while framing charges, is not to act \u201cmerely as a Post Office or a mouth-piece of the prosecution, but has to consider the broad probabilities of the case, the total effect of the evidence and the documents produced before the Court, any basic infirmities appearing in the case and so on\u201d.106<\/a><\/sup>Union of India v. Prafulla Kumar Samal<\/em>, (1979) 3 SCC 4, at para 10.\u00a0<\/span><\/p>\n

    Thus, for example, if the police have invoked obscenity provisions in a case of NCIID, where the real harm is the violation of privacy and consent, the court can choose to not proceed under the obscenity provisions, and instead charge the accused for offenses under Section 354C of the IPC and Section 66E of the IT Act.<\/p>\n

    Unfortunately, as discussed in the Typologies module, courts, in such cases, also frequently invoke obscenity provisions, which do not address the harms or rights violations suffered by victims. As the reasoning offered in such cases shows, courts are more concerned with whether the morphed image will deprave or corrupt an individual viewing it.<\/p>\n

    Courts also invoke Sections that seek redressal for the outrage of a woman\u2019s modesty (Sections 354 and 509 of the IPC). As discussed in the Typologies module, when modesty provisions are invoked, they tend to be used by the court and lawyers to pass paternalistic and moralistic comments about a woman\u2019s conduct and deny her remedy if she does not fit the ideal victim<\/i> imaginary.107<\/a><\/sup>Satish, M. (2016). Discretion, Discrimination and the Rule of Law: Reforming Rape Sentencing in India<\/i>. Cambridge University Press. <\/span><\/p>\n

    Framing of appropriate charges is, therefore, crucial to ensure that the harm and rights violations suffered by the survivor are rightly addressed, without unwarranted judgments being made about her conduct and social standing, and without inadvertently exposing her to criminal charges.<\/p>\n

    3.2.3.2 Bail conditions<\/h4>\n

    Most sections under the IPC that are used in OGBV cases are bailable in nature, except Section 354 (assault of or use of criminal force on a woman with the intent to outrage her modesty) which is used commonly in cases of sexual harassment.<\/p>\n

    While bail in criminal cases has to be the rule, courts often use sexist and patriarchal reasoning to grant bail in OGBV cases.108<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a><\/span> For instance, in a case involving NCIID and blackmail, the court\u2019s reasoning for granting bail contained a strong reference to the fact that the prosecutrix, a married woman, had consensual sex with the accused and that there was a delay of more than a year in filing the complaint.110<\/a><\/sup>Anand Mohan v. State of Bihar<\/em>, Crl. M. No. 35856 (2020), order dated 24 June 2021, Patna High Court.<\/span><\/p>\n

    Another issue in granting bail is that bail conditions do not reflect a sufficient understanding of the online public sphere and the role of technology in mediating violence, and are inadequate to reduce harm to the victim while the accused is at large.111<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst with Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases<\/i> (Draft Report), pp. 91-113. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a><\/span> Bail orders are not customized to the unique requirements of OGBV cases and impose the same conditions as cases where the harm is not technologically inflicted.112<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst with Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases<\/i> (Draft Report), pp. 91-113. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a><\/span><\/p>\n

    Further, we also need to caution against a disturbing trend in bail conditions imposed in gender-based violence cases, where the accused and the prosecutrix are asked to compromise or the former is asked to tie rakhi<\/i> \u2014 an amulet or talisman tied around the wrist of a man typically on a Hindu festival to set up brotherly relations113<\/a><\/sup>Wikipedia. (n.d.). Raksha Bandhan<\/i>. https:\/\/en.wikipedia.org\/wiki\/Raksha_Bandhan<\/a> <\/span> \u2014 to the latter, thereby enabling their contact, which can lead to trauma and revictimization of the survivor.114<\/a><\/sup>Vikram v.     3.2.3.3 Difficulty in proving guilt and evidentiary value of testimony<\/h4>\n

    Cases of OGBV are mostly dealt with under the criminal jurisdiction of courts, as they constitute offenses under the IPC and the IT Act. As a result, the burden of proof is on the prosecution to offer adequate evidence to establish the guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubt.115<\/a><\/sup>In criminal law cases, the burden of proof is expected to be \u201cbeyond reasonable doubt\u201d of guilt of the accused, see University of Minnesota. (n.d.). Criminal Law<\/i>. https:\/\/open.lib.umn.edu\/criminallaw\/chapter\/2-4-the-burden-of-proof\/<\/a><\/span> However, our study found that several cases of OGBV fail to meet the high burden of proof because of hindrances such as bringing in expert testimony, relying on witnesses, and offering the right documentary evidence, among others.116<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst with Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases (Draft Report), pp.82-82. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a><\/span><\/p>\n

    Another hurdle is the court\u2019s treatment of the victim\u2019s testimony. The Supreme Court\u2019s ruling that a conviction may be based solely on the victim\u2019s testimony117<\/a><\/sup>Phool Singh v. State of Madhya Pradesh<\/em>, (2022) 2 SCC 74, at para 5. <\/span> is conditional on such testimony being sterling<\/i>, meaning that it must be unassailable and consistent throughout the court process.118<\/a><\/sup>Phool Singh v. State of Madhya Pradesh<\/em>, (2022) 2 SCC 74, at para 5. <\/span> Although the Supreme Court simultaneously clarified that minor contradictions would not suffice to disregard a testimony, defense lawyers routinely and successfully, seek to poke holes in a victim\u2019s testimony through a barrage of questions and by creating an environment that is hostile to the victim. The Supreme Court\u2019s expectation that a sterling witness must \u201cwithstand the cross-examination of any length and however strenuous\u201d119<\/a><\/sup>Rai Sandeep v. State (NCT of Delhi)<\/em>, (2012) 8 SCC 21, at para 22.\u00a0<\/span> is problematic in light of the trauma and fear faced by victims during a testimony. What\u2019s more? Judges base the credibility of a woman\u2019s testimony on the extent to which she fits the paradigm of an ideal victim<\/i>.120<\/a><\/sup>Agnes, F. (1992). Protecting Women against Violence? Review of a Decade of Legislation. Economic and Political Weekly<\/i>, 27<\/em>(17), pp. 19-33.; Kapur, R., & Cossman, B. (1996). Subversive Sites: Feminist Engagement with Law in India<\/i>, p.121. Sage Publications.<\/span> IT for Change\u2019s study documented several such instances. For example, in a rape case with elements of OGBV, one of the reasons cited by the court to grant bail to the accused was that the victim had failed to report the crime immediately, which the court deemed not \u201cbecoming\u201d of a rape victim.121<\/a><\/sup>Sri Rakesh B. v. State of Karnataka<\/em>, Crl. P. No. 2427, judgment dated 22 June 2020, Karnataka High Court.<\/span><\/p>\n

    3.2.3.4 Admission of digital evidence<\/h4>\n

    The difficulty of proving guilt is compounded by the unique challenges faced in the production and admissibility of electronic or digital evidence. Such evidence is crucial in OGBV cases since the record of wrongdoing or harm is captured on a device or the internet.<\/p>\n

    Admission of digital evidence is governed by Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act. Section 65B(1) of the Act differentiates between an original<\/i> electronic record contained in a device in which the information was first stored and secondary copies made from the original which may be produced (example, handwritten copies) or printed on a paper, and stored, recorded, or copied in optical or magnetic media. The former can be directly adduced as evidence if the owner of the device steps into the witness box and proves that the concerned device, on which the original information was first stored, is owned and\/or operated by them.122<\/a><\/sup>Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal<\/em>, (2020) 7 SCC 1, at para 31-32.<\/span> The latter can be admitted as evidence only if it meets certain requirements specified by Section 65B (2), and more importantly, if a certificate is produced in accordance with Section 65B(4), which identifies the electronic record, and gives particulars of the device involved in the production of the electronic record.123<\/a><\/sup> Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Section 65B(4)(a). <\/span> The certificate has to be given by the person occupying a responsible official position in relation to the operation of the relevant device, such as the owner.124<\/a><\/sup>The Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Section 65B(4)(c). <\/span> This certification requirement is \u201cto ensure the source and authenticity of the electronic evidence, which are the two hallmarks pertaining to electronic record sought to be used as evidence\u201d.125<\/a><\/sup>Anvar P.V. v. P.K Basheer and Ors<\/em>, (2014) 1 SCC (LS) 108, at para 16.<\/span><\/p>\n

    In most cases of OGBV involving electronic evidence, the production of secondary evidence is more feasible than primary evidence because of the difficulty or sometimes the impracticability of bringing into the court the computer system or computer network where the electronic evidence was first stored. However, the requirement of certification poses hurdles in admitting secondary copies of electronic records as evidence before the court. In some cases, it may not be possible to obtain the certificate if the litigant is not in possession of the electronic device storing the electronic document. Sometimes despite applying to the authority holding the electronic document, a certificate under Section 65B(4) may not be available.126<\/a><\/sup>Sekhri, A. (2020). The Supreme Court, 65-B Certificates, and Electronic Evidence. <\/i>The Proof of Guilt. https:\/\/theproofofguilt.blogspot.com\/2020\/07\/the-supreme-court-65-b-certificates-and.html<\/a><\/span> In other cases, the litigant may not be aware of who the concerned authority is to issue such a certificate.127<\/a><\/sup>Sekhri, A. (2020). The Supreme Court, 65-B Certificates, and Electronic Evidence. <\/i>The Proof of Guilt. https:\/\/theproofofguilt.blogspot.com\/2020\/07\/the-supreme-court-65-b-certificates-and.html<\/a><\/span><\/p>\n

    Our study of OGBV cases heard in trial courts across Delhi and Bengaluru found that electronic evidence was often not considered due to the lack of the requisite certification.128<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst with Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases<\/i> (Draft Report), pp. 87-88. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a>. <\/span> In State by Sampigehalli Police Station, Bengaluru v. Faiz Ahamed (2016),<\/i>129<\/a><\/sup>State by Sampigehalli Police Station, Bengaluru v. Faiz Ahamed,<\/em> C.C. No. 5392. (2012.), judgmjent dated 27 September 2016, Bangalore District Court. <\/span> a case of non-consensual capture of intimate image and intimidation, electronic evidence was provided through compact discs (CDs), copies of email, color photocopies of the photographs, and printouts. However, since the prosecution had not produced the certificate for secondary evidence under Section 65B(4), the trial court refused to admit the evidence and acquitted the accused. This is only one instance of how strict application of the certification requirement could make the production and admission of secondary electronic evidence prohibitively difficult for victims of OGBV.130<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst with Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases<\/i> (Draft Report), pp. 87-88. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a>\u00a0<\/span><\/p>\n

    In light of these difficulties, the Supreme Court, in Shafhi Mohammed v. State of Himachal Pradesh (<\/i>2018)<\/i>, held that certification under Section 65B(4) is not mandatory and can be dispensed with \u201cin the interest of justice\u201d in cases where the device is inaccessible to the person seeking to obtain the certificate (such as where it is in adverse possession).131<\/a><\/sup>Shafhi Mohammed v. To mitigate the rigors of the provision, the court ruled that a person unable to access a device or obtain a certificate must approach the court, which is empowered to order the production of an electronic record under Section 165 of the Indian Evidence Act and other provisions of civil and criminal procedure codes.132<\/a><\/sup>Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. In this way, the procedural difficulties are sought to be mitigated by the exercise of the court\u2019s power to summon evidence and the necessary certificate.<\/p>\n

    Apart from the issue of admissibility of secondary electronic records, our study also found that courts, in certain cases, did not consider electronic evidence even when it was present.133<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst with Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases<\/i> (Draft Report), pp. 88-89. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a> <\/span> In other cases, the prosecution failed to produce such secondary evidence.134<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst with Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases<\/i> (Draft Report), pp. 88-89. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a> <\/span> This is concerning as electronic evidence is a crucial part of OGBV cases, without which justice is impossible to achieve.<\/p>\n

Additionally, the Supreme Court asked all courts to be sensitive towards victims in cases of gender-based violence to avoid retraumatization during proceedings and arguments. This is a welcome judgment from the highest court in the country and needs to be incorporated by the judiciary in letter and spirit. Additionally, in August 2023, the Supreme Court of India released a Handbook on Combating Gender Stereotypes.148<\/a><\/sup>Handbook on Combating Gender Stereotypes<\/em>. (2023). It also identifies common stereotypes about women and demonstrates why they are inaccurate and how they may distort the application of the law.149<\/a><\/sup>Handbook on Combating Gender Stereotypes<\/em>. (2023). Supreme Court of India. https:\/\/main.sci.gov.in\/pdf\/LU\/16082023_073106.pdf<\/a><\/span><\/p>\n

    Going forward, courts\u2019 discussions on consent and the sexual agency of women need to be analyzed through a feminist lens. In certain cases, courts have been unable to acknowledge women\u2019s sexual agency and have, instead, adopted a protectionist approach.150<\/a><\/sup>State of Punjab v. Gurmeet Singh,<\/i> (1996) 2 SCC 384. <\/span> In others, courts have ignored multiple consents that may be involved in OGBV cases like NCIID. There needs to be an appreciation by the judiciary of the fact that privacy is often contextually implicated and that active consent faces challenges in light of social and gender power that vitiates free choice.151<\/a><\/sup>Gurumurthy, A., Vasudevan, A., & Chami, N. (2018). Examining Technology-Mediated Violence against Women through a Feminist Framework: Towards Appropriate Legal-Institutional Responses in India<\/i>.     3.2.3.6 Redressal from the court<\/h4>\n

    If a criminal prosecution leads to conviction, the end result is either imprisonment and\/or the imposition of a fine on the accused. While this meets the ends of justice in some sense, it is doubtful if it really redresses the harm caused to a survivor of OGBV if the offensive image or content, around which the case is centered, is still up on the internet and open to be shared with anyone. Many instances of OGBV occur on platforms of internet intermediaries, such as social media, which are in a position to remove the offensive content. In fact, Section 79 of the IT Act, as interpreted by the Supreme Court in Shreya Singhal<\/i> v.<\/i> Union of India (2015),<\/i>152<\/a><\/sup>Shreya Singhal v. Union of India, <\/i>(2015) 5 SCC 1. <\/span> empowers courts to issue directions to intermediaries to remove illegal content from their platform.153<\/a><\/sup>Shreya Singhal v. Union of India, <\/i>(2015) 5 SCC 1, at para 117. <\/span> As per proviso 2 of Rule 3(1)(d) of the IT Rules, upon receipt of a court order to take down content, the intermediary is required to do so within 36 hours.154<\/a><\/sup>Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code), 2021, Second proviso to Rule 3(1)(d).<\/span> If it fails to comply with such an order, the safe harbor exemption available to intermediaries under Section 79(1) of the IT Act is forfeited, and the intermediary entity and its officers can be held liable for action as mandated by Section 85 of the IT Act.155<\/a><\/sup> Information Technology Act, 2000, Section 79(3)<\/span><\/p>\n

    IT for Change\u2019s study found that courts recognized the role of social media platforms or relevant intermediaries and requested their cooperation to remove the offensive content in only a few cases.156<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst With Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases (Draft Report),<\/em> pp. 114-128. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a> <\/span> In fact, orders to take down offensive content should not wait till the end of the trial, as is sometimes the case, since the continued presence of such content online during the trial period can cause significant harm to the complainant.157<\/a><\/sup>X v. Union Of India And Ors<\/em>, W.P.(Crl) 1082 (2020) & Crl.M.A. Nos. 9485. (2020)., 10986-87. (2020), judgment dated 20 April 2021, Supreme Court of India, at para 8. <\/span> In a particular case from our study sample, the accused persons continued to repost and redirect the offending content from one website to another, and from one online platform to another during the course of the trial.158<\/a><\/sup>X v. Union Of India And Ors<\/em>, W.P.(Crl) 1082 (2020) & Crl.M.A. Nos. 9485. (2020)., 10986-87. (2020), judgment dated 20 April 2021, Supreme Court of India, at para 8. <\/span> It is crucial, therefore, that courts issue interim orders directing the removal of such content while the litigation is still underway. Given the challenges of cross-functioning between the state, the intermediaries, including social media platforms and websites, and the court, the intermediaries should be included as parties in the trial to ease the process of effectively enforcing content takedown orders.159<\/a><\/sup>Rajkumar, M., & Sen, S. (2022). The Indian Judiciary's Tryst With Online Gender-Based Violence: An Empirical Analysis of Indian Cases (Draft Report)<\/em>, pp. 121-124. IT for Change. https:\/\/itforchange.net\/sites\/default\/files\/2190\/ITfC-Draft-Case-Law-Research-Report_1.pdf<\/a>\u00a0<\/span> Currently, the intermediaries are not included as parties, thereby impeding the ability of complainants to get the illegal content removed from the internet.<\/p>\n

    What can the court do?<\/h3>\n
      \n
    1. The court should apply its independent judicial mind to the police report and frame charges appropriate to the harm and rights violations suffered by the victim. Judges must avoid taking a protectionist approach and be open to creative applications of the IT Act and IPC provisions that emphasize women\u2019s dignity, privacy, and autonomy. Further, Section 216 of CrPC empowers the court to alter or add any charge at any time before a judgment is pronounced. This provision may be used in an OGBV case to add an appropriate charge, even at a later stage, if it becomes evident that the original charges framed are not suitable to provide justice to the victim and address the harm suffered by her.<\/li>\n
    2. Courts must follow the guidelines laid down by the Supreme Court in Aparna Bhat v. State of Madhya Pradesh (2021)<\/i>160<\/a><\/sup>Aparna Bhat v. State of Madhya Pradesh<\/em>, (2021) 4 SCR 479, at para 44. <\/span> and the Handbook on Combating Gender Stereotypes161<\/a><\/sup>Handbook on Combating Gender Stereotypes. (2023). Supreme Court of India. https:\/\/main.sci.gov.in\/pdf\/LU\/16082023_073106.pdf<\/a><\/span> while dealing with sexual crimes. These guidelines include the direction that bail conditions and court orders should avoid relying on stereotypical and patriarchal notions about women and their place in society and must be strictly in accordance with the requirements of the CrPC.162<\/a><\/sup>Aparna Bhat v. State of Madhya Pradesh<\/em>, (2021) 4 SCR 479, at para 44.<\/span> In other words, discussions about the dress, behavior, or past conduct or morals of the prosecutrix should not be a part of the verdict granting bail. The Supreme Court also directed that bail conditions should not mandate, require, or permit contact, compromise, or mediation between the accused and the victim.163<\/a><\/sup>Aparna Bhat v. State of Madhya Pradesh<\/em>, (2021) 4 SCR 479, at para 44. <\/span> Instead, these conditions should seek to protect the complainant from any further harassment by the accused. Further, courts should customize bail orders and conditions to meet the unique requirements of OGBV cases, and the peculiarities of the internet, social media platforms, and technology\/communication devices. Further, the guidelines laid down in the Aparna Bhatt case should prompt courts to be sensitive towards victims of gender-based violence cases to avoid retraumatization during the proceedings and arguments.164<\/a><\/sup>Aparna Bhat v. State of Madhya Pradesh<\/em>, (2021) 4 SCR 479, at para 44.<\/span> Courts should base their decisions on the law and facts in evidence and not engage in gender stereotyping.<\/i>165<\/a><\/sup> Aparna Bhat v. State of Madhya Pradesh<\/em>, (2021) 4 SCR 479, at para 44.<\/span> The same sensitivity should be displayed in examining the evidentiary value of the victim\u2019s testimony. In this regard, the Supreme Court directed courts to steer clear of the following stereotypes:\n
        \n
      • \u201cWomen are emotional and often overreact or dramatize events, hence it is necessary to corroborate their testimony;<\/li>\n
      • testimonial evidence provided by women who are sexually active may be suspected when assessing \u2018consent\u2019 in sexual offence cases.\u201d166<\/a><\/sup>Aparna Bhat v. State of Madhya Pradesh,<\/i> (2021) 4 SCR 479, at para 44. <\/span><\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/li>\n
      • In light of the Supreme Court\u2019s ruling in the Arjun Panditrao case,167<\/a><\/sup>Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal,<\/i> (2020) 7 SCC 1. <\/span> courts should not dismiss secondary digital evidence if the prosecution is unable to obtain the certificate under Section 65B(4) of the Indian Evidence Act despite making the necessary efforts. Instead, it should exercise its powers under the CrPC to direct the concerned person to call for evidence and direct the production of the certificate. This can, at a preliminary level, prevent evidence from being dismissed in the first instance. It is necessary that courts enable the admission of digital evidence and recognize its challenges.<\/li>\n<\/ol>\n

        Where appropriate, courts should issue orders to relevant internet intermediaries to take down the offending content to prevent and mitigate harm from OGBV. If the content is prima facie<\/em> found to be illegal, such takedown orders should be issued as soon as possible as interim orders to prevent the further circulation of the content online. Further, where required, courts should also make internet intermediaries parties to the case, for instance, when it is found that the algorithms of intermediaries made the offensive content viral, in order to provide an effective remedy to the victim.<\/p>\n<\/div>\n<\/div><\/div><\/div>
